I have spent a lot of my life trying to figure out whether I am an engineer or a scientist.

My job title currently says engineer. I have a PhD. I was raised by a software engineer, then spent the next decade training under scientists. That is not a clean identity graph. It is more like a merge conflict that has been open for years.

That is why _Project Hail Mary_ hit harder than I expected.

On the surface, it is a fun hard-science survival story. Underneath, it is one of the better pop-culture illustrations I have seen of the difference between engineering intelligence and scientific intelligence. Not because one is better. Because they are different kinds of power.

And that difference matters more now that we are building non-human engineers.

Spoilers for _Project Hail Mary_ below.

## Rocky Is An Engineer

Rocky is almost unfairly optimized for engineering.

He perceives structure directly. He can understand shape through sound. He can fabricate. He can repair. He can translate a physical problem into a working artifact with terrifying speed. He does not need sleep. He runs on an alien metabolism that feels, in the context of this essay, suspiciously close to electricity.

He is not magical because he is abstractly smart. He is magical because he is embodied competence.

Something is broken. Rocky builds a fix.

Something is unknown. Rocky instruments it.

Something needs to connect to something else. Rocky finds the interface.

That is engineering at its best: a ruthless intimacy with constraints. Materials, energy, failure modes, interfaces, tolerances. Good engineering is not vague cleverness. It is the ability to make reality accept your proposal.

This is also the part of engineering that AI agents are coming for first.

Not because agents already beat great engineers today in every context. They do not. But because so much engineering work rewards patience, breadth, repetition, and exhaustive checking. The best engineers are often the most thorough people in the room. They read the docs. They trace the failure. They test the weird path. They keep going after everyone else is bored.

That is exactly the kind of labor machines are structurally advantaged at.

Agents will not sleep. They will not get tired of checking one more log. They will not resent the fourth refactor. They will not need the work to feel elegant. Give them electricity, tools, memory, tests, and enough feedback, and they will grind through the implementation surface.

That should make engineers uncomfortable.

It should not make humans feel useless.

## Grace Is A Scientist

Ryland Grace is not valuable because he can out-engineer Rocky.

He often cannot.

His value is that he can connect strange facts across domains. He can live inside ambiguity without freezing. He can notice that the important problem is not always the obvious one. He can make conceptual jumps before the measurements fully justify them.

That is science at its best.

Science is not just lab coats and papers. It is the discipline of asking reality better questions. It is deciding what might matter before there is a clean spec. It is choosing the right abstraction when the data is incomplete and the stakes are high.

Engineering asks: how do we make this work?

Science asks: what is actually happening?

Those questions overlap, but they are not the same question.

At the end of the book, Grace is teaching Rocky's species. That image stuck with me. The hyper-competent engineer civilization still needs the scientist. Not because they lack intelligence, but because intelligence pointed at construction is different from intelligence pointed at explanation.

The engineer can build the bridge.

The scientist can change what everyone believes a bridge is for.

## Hamming's Question Still Hurts

Richard Hamming's "You and Your Research" keeps resurfacing for a reason. The famous pressure in that talk is not productivity advice. It is moral pressure.

What are the important problems in your field?

Are you working on them?

Why not?

That lands differently in an agentic world.

If execution gets cheaper, then avoiding the important problem gets harder to justify. "I did not have time to build it" becomes weaker when you can point a swarm of tireless engineers at the build. The scarce thing shifts upward: choosing the problem, framing the experiment, deciding what evidence would change your mind, noticing which assumptions everyone else inherited.

Hamming also talks about ambiguity as a trait of great scientists. That feels central here. Engineering cultures often say they value ambiguity, but a lot of engineering work tries to destroy it: write the ticket, pin the interface, define the contract, close the issue.

That is good. That is how things ship.

But science begins before the ticket can be written.

It begins in the fog, where the nouns are unstable.

## The Agent Future Is Not Post-Human

The lazy version of this argument is "engineers are obsolete."

I do not believe that.

The better version is that engineering identity has to move up the stack.

For a long time, being good at implementation was enough to create enormous value. You knew the tools. You could hold the system in your head. You could turn an idea into running code. That skill still matters, but its monopoly is breaking.

The next durable advantage is not typing the code. It is knowing what should exist.

It is taste.

It is problem selection.

It is being able to tell when the agent built exactly what you asked for and still missed the point.

It is knowing which constraint is real and which one is inherited ceremony.

It is connecting a half-remembered biology paper, a weird production failure, a customer complaint, and a half-formed tool idea into one new question.

That is where science and engineering recombine.

The scientist without engineering cannot touch the world. The engineer without science becomes a local optimizer. The interesting work is in the loop: ask, build, observe, revise.

Agents make the build step cheaper. They do not remove the need for the loop.

They may make it more important.

## Project Hail Agent

Maybe the title is _Project Hail Agent_. Maybe it should be _Project Hail Claude_. The pun matters less than the discomfort underneath it.

Rocky is what we are trying to build: tireless, practical, fluent in the material of his world, able to turn constraints into artifacts.

Grace is what we still need to be: confused in productive ways, able to cross domains, stubborn about important questions, willing to sit with ambiguity long enough for a new model to appear.

I do not think the future belongs to pure engineers or pure scientists.

It belongs to people who can command engineers that do not sleep, while still doing the human work of deciding what is worth building.

That is a stranger job.

It is also, maybe, a better one.
