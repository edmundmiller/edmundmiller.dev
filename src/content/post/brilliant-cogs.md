I have spent my career in startups. Working beside a dominant genomics company changed how I judged the people inside it.

Talented engineers can become brilliant cogs in a company whose incentives do not reward a better product. A poor product does not prove that the people building it lack skill or care.

## The genomics context

[SanDiegOmics estimated](https://sandiegomics.com/2023-sequencing-market-share-the-tide-is-turning-very-slowly/) that Illumina held 77.6% of the sequencing market in 2023. That figure described a concentrated market, not a legal finding of monopoly.

Concentration changes the pressure on a company. A customer with expensive instruments, validated workflows, trained staff, and years of data cannot switch vendors quickly. Vertical integration can raise that cost further when instruments, consumables, and software depend on one another.

In that environment, a weak software experience may not cost the company a sale. Product quality exerts less pressure than it would in a market where customers can leave easily.

## What changed my view

Working at a smaller company introduced me to former employees of the dominant vendor. Many were thoughtful engineers with deep knowledge of the field. Their work made it harder to blame individuals for every frustrating product decision.

The archived essay [AWS Tools Suck](https://web.archive.org/web/20240521123134/https://www.cyclic.sh/posts/aws-tools-suck/) helped me name the pattern. Its argument about the services built around AWS was an analogy, not evidence about genomics. The useful idea was that a platform's incentives shape its developer experience.

This is a personal reflection, not a complete economic analysis. One market-share estimate and my experience cannot establish every cause behind a product decision.

The narrower lesson still matters. Before judging the engineer behind a poor product, examine the company's constraints and rewards. Brilliant cogs do not choose the direction of the whole machine.
